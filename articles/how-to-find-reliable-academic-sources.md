# How to Find Reliable Academic Sources

Most people trust academic sources for the wrong reasons - logos, platforms, or AI citations - then get blindsided by bias or dead links. The core insight is that **reliability comes from process, not appearance**. You will learn how to spot traps, check sources, and build habits that make reliability the default.

### TL;DR:
* Ignore logos and platform labels; check the source’s process and funding.
* Never trust AI-generated citations without verifying the original.
* Run searches in at least two library databases to catch gaps.
* Cross-check every key claim with at least two independent sources.
* Flag any source you can’t trace to a real author or institution.

---

## 1. Define Reliability by Process, Not Appearance
Most users mistake surface cues for reliability. A `.edu` domain or a polished logo feels safe, but these are easily faked. 

**Lateral Reading vs. Vertical Reading:**
* **Vertical Reading:** Staying on one site and accepting its self-presentation. This is a major mistake.
* **Lateral Reading:** Fact-checkers outperform others by opening new tabs to check reputation, funding, and author background. This is the strongest predictor of source reliability.



## 2. Avoid AI Citation Traps and Trust Biases
AI tools (like Grok, Gemini, or Perplexity) are notorious for "hallucinations" - linking to error pages or fabricating articles. In recent tests, some AI models linked to dead pages in over 75% of citations.

> **Rule of Thumb:** Never copy citations from AI without checking the original. "Trust kills verification" - the more comfortable a platform makes you feel, the more likely you are to overlook industry ties or conflicts of interest.

## 3. Check Peer Review, But Don’t Stop There
Peer review is a baseline, not a guarantee. Even reputable journals publish flawed studies, and "predatory journals" mimic the peer-review process to collect fees.

* **The Check:** Confirm the journal is indexed in recognized databases like **PubMed** or **Web of Science**. 
* **The Red Flag:** Avoid journals that solicit submissions via spam email or promise "rapid publication" for a high fee.



## 4. Use Multiple Library Databases to Spot Gaps
No single database is complete. Using tools like **JSTOR**, **Scopus**, or **PubMed** narrows your search to credible content, but you should always run your search across at least two major databases. 

**A Practical Script:**
1. Search your topic in two databases.
2. Compare top results for overlap.
3. Open the original PDF, not just the abstract.
4. Check for retractions on the publisher’s site.

## 5. Verify Author Credentials and Funding
A strong source lists the author’s full name and institutional affiliation. 
* **Scrutiny:** Search the author’s name plus their institution. If they have no trace in academic circles or a mismatched affiliation, treat the source as suspect.
* **Funding:** Industry-funded research isn't automatically invalid, but it requires extra scrutiny for bias.

## 6. Spotting Predatory Journals and Fake Publishers
Predatory journals mimic legitimate outlets. Before trusting a journal, check for:
* Inclusion in the **DOAJ** (Directory of Open Access Journals).
* A clear editorial board of verifiable scholars.
* Transparent retraction and correction policies.

If a journal seems unfamiliar, search the name plus "predatory" or check **Beall’s List** and similar watchdogs.
